# Camera Setups & Rigs — Directorial Translation Reference

Reference vocabulary for converting emotion-flavored shot-list prose into **specific editorial instructions** for cinematic video-gen ads (action, western, space, sports, automotive, luxury). Pull from this table to replace feelings with the physical setups that photograph those feelings.

## How to write a directive from a row

Anatomy: **`[setup + lock point]: [where the rig physically is] , [what stays locked] , [what the world does around it]`**

Canonical target voice:

> "single body-rig snorricam locked on the right earcup: camera bolted to his body, the headphones rock solid dead center, everything behind him ripping past in motion blur."

Rules for the translating agent:

1. **Name the setup first.** Rig names ("snorricam", "FPV drone", "dolly zoom", "handheld") are strong conditioning tokens for video-gen models — always pair the name with its visible consequence.
2. **State the physical placement** — what the camera is bolted to, where it sits relative to subject.
3. **Lock one thing, move everything else** (face, product, horizon). The lock/motion contrast IS the effect.
4. **Translate emotion → mechanism** using the index below; never leave an emotion word doing a verb's job.
5. **Stack sparingly:** one rig + one time treatment max (e.g. "russian-arm orbit + speed ramp through the apex").
6. Keep directives ≤ 35 words, in the sample-directive voice.

## Emotion → setup index

| The prompt says… | Reach for |
|---|---|
| awe, wonder, "breathtaking" | spielberg face, villeneuve scale wide, crane-up reveal, heli gyro-ball |
| dread, menace, "something's wrong" | kubrick one-point push, dolly zoom, splash box below-waterline, low-mode steadicam, fincher locked |
| panic, unraveling, disorientation | snorricam, handheld verité, scott crash zooms, whip pan |
| adrenaline, speed, "heart-pounding" | FPV drone oner, russian arm, gimbal oner + handoffs, speed ramp, crash cam |
| triumph, heroism, "rises to the moment" | bayhem hero-rise orbit, crane-up, arc/orbit, bullet time |
| seduction, luxury, "effortless elegance" | steadicam oner, slider micro-move, moco arm + high-speed, probe lens, deakins silhouette |
| isolation, longing, "left behind" | dolly pull-back, step-printing smear, double dolly, villeneuve scale wide |
| tension, standoff, "the moment before" | leone standoff ladder, split diopter, dolly push-in creep |
| intimacy, connection, honesty | demme direct-to-camera CU, hostess tray profile, process-trailer two-hander |
| chaos with clarity, "war zone" | kurosawa telephoto layers, gimbal oner + handoffs, handheld narrow-shutter |
| whimsy, precision, deadpan charm | planimetric framing, 90° whip pans, overhead god's-eye insert |
| nostalgia, memory, "golden" | malick magic-hour steadicam, step-printing smear |
| fate, omniscience, inevitability | overhead god's-eye, fincher locked camera, double dolly, kubrick push |
| impact, "the hit lands" | crash cam, speed ramp, bullet time, crash zoom |

| BODY | Helmet / jaw POV rig | Camera locked to the performer's head at jaw height so their own hands and chest stay in frame; deliberate head-bob retained | Full first-person — hands punch into frame, world swings with every glance | FPS adrenaline, extreme sports, "you are the driver" *(Hardcore Henry)* | Jaw-mount POV: her gloves rack the bolt in-frame, horizon lurching with each stride down the service stairs. |

| VEHICLE | Hood mount | Camera hard-rigged to the picture car's hood/chassis, triangulated to kill wobble; frames back through the windshield or forward off the nose | Frame welded to the chassis — every bump is the car's own body language; actors rock inside a rock-stable frame | In-car tension at speed, road-trip montage, Bullitt-lineage road POV | Hood-mount through the windshield: rock-steady two-shot while the suspension bucks them, sodium lights streaming up the glass. |
| VEHICLE | Hostess tray (door mount) | Side platform slung on the car door at window height; camera leveled on a dovetail, suctioned and strapped to the body | Locked profile close-up of the driver — face steady, background streaking past the glass | Driver isolation, dialogue-at-speed, reaction + speed blur | Hostess tray on the driver's door: her profile locked at window height, city smearing behind her jaw, hands loose on the wheel. |
| VEHICLE | Process trailer (tow rig, low loader) | Picture car rides a low trailer towed by a crew cab; actors pretend-drive; deck carries crew, lights, even a mini-crane arcing around the car | Calm controllable dialogue at apparent speed — real passing light, actors fully free to perform | Emotional two-handers in cars, luxury-interior beats at speed | Process-trailer move: mini-crane arcs from her window across the hood to his — conversation continuous, boulevard sliding past. |
| VEHICLE | Biscuit rig (pod car) | Self-powered platform carrying the picture car; hidden pod driver actually drives while the actor works a dummy wheel | The star visibly drives hard — wind, G-forces, vibration on their face, zero visible support | Racing hero moments where the face must sell the speed *(Ford v Ferrari)* | Biscuit rig at pace: pod driver buried low, hero "driving" flat-out — camera arm swings from grille to his gritted teeth in one move. |

| VEHICLE | Crash cam (expendable hard-mount) | Cheap stripped camera body bolted directly in harm's way — under the jump ramp, on the roll cage, at the impact point | The hit itself, full frame, no flinch — a frame the audience knows shouldn't exist | Impact punctuation inside action beats *(Fury Road)* | Crash cam bolted at the landing point: undercarriage fills frame, gravel spraying the lens on impact — hold till whiteout. |
| AERIAL | FPV drone oner | Racing quad with a stripped cinema camera hard-mounted — no gimbal, moves 1:1 with a goggled race pilot flying acro | Kamikaze single take: dives tower glass, threads railings, flies under moving cars — impossible-but-real | Adrenaline oners, spatial reveals connecting geography in one breath *(Ambulance)* | FPV dive off the tower roof: full-tilt drop down the glass, level out through the parking-garage gap, exit under the moving truck. |
| AERIAL | Heavy-lift cinema drone | Large multirotor flying the production's actual A-camera and cine glass on a 3-axis head; pilot + separate gimbal op + wireless focus | Helicopter-grade silk that intercuts invisibly with ground coverage; precise low-altitude lines no heli can fly | Premium establishing sweeps, low-over-water passes, aerials matching the A-cam look | Heavy-lift drone ten feet off the water: hold her wake dead-center, then rise on axis until the coastline resolves. |
| AERIAL | Cable cam (wire cam; Skycam/Spidercam class) | Camera carriage riding tensioned cables — point-to-point line or four-winch 3D flight over stadium/set; stabilized head below | Silent floating glide on an exact repeatable path; hovers where drones are banned | Stadium/crowd flyovers, point-to-point runs, repeatable VFX passes *(NFL, Eras Tour)* | Wire-cam run goal-to-goal: drop from rafter height to shoulder height over the crowd's reaching hands, land on the podium beat. |
| AERIAL | Helicopter gyro-ball (stabilized nose/ball mount) | Spherical gyro-stabilized head with long zoom slung on a heli hardpoint; operator flies the lens from a console, independent of aircraft attitude | Vast horizon-locked sweeps; long-lens push from miles out to a face on a rooftop, silk through turbulence | Epic establishing, air-to-air action, city-at-night luxury *(Top Gun: Maverick)* | Heli ball-mount at altitude: start on the full mesa, long-lens creep across two miles until the lone rider fills frame. |
| CRANE | Technocrane compound move | Telescoping crane arm (10–100 ft) with stabilized remote head; extends/retracts mid-swing, arc-compensated for dead-straight lines | Camera snakes through windows, over crowds, lands on a close-up — one unbroken impossible path | Epic establishing pulls, one-shot scale flexes, hero reveals | 50-ft technocrane: start tight on the glass in her hand, telescope out and up over the terrace crowd to the full skyline — one move. |
| CRANE | Jib vertical (crane-up reveal / crane-down "god descends") | Counterweighted boom on a fixed or dolly base; fixed arm length so every move is an arc — rise to a high wide, or sink from overhead into the scene | Weightless rise shrinks the hero into a widening world; descent feels like judgment arriving | Triumph vs verdict beats, crowd-scale reveals *(Gone with the Wind railyard)* | Crane-up from his shoulders: hold his stillness while the frame lifts to reveal ten thousand behind him. |
| CRANE | Dolly push-in (Spielberg push, slow creep) | Camera on track or dance-floor dolly; grip creeps it toward the subject inches per minute | Frame tightens almost subliminally — the audience is pulled inside the decision forming | Resolve crystallizing, dawning dread, the game-face beat *(The Godfather)* | Six-inch creep across the whole speech: start loose at the shoulders, end at the eyes as she says yes. |
| CRANE | Dolly pull-back reveal | Same dolly-on-track run in reverse — camera retreats and the frame widens | Subject shrinks inside growing context — isolation, aftermath, or the ironic reveal | Loneliness beats, aftermath, end-of-spot product-in-world *(The Shining typewriter)* | Slow pull-back from the trophy on the bench: widen until the empty locker room swallows it. |
| CRANE | Dolly zoom (vertigo shot, zolly, trombone) | Dolly toward subject while zooming out (or reverse), rates matched so the subject holds exact size; only the background warps | Perspective itself buckles — floor-drops-away unreality around a locked face | Shock realization, the-world-shifts moments — deploy once *(Jaws beach, Vertigo)* | Dolly-zoom on his beach chair: face locked mid-frame while the shoreline stretches away behind him. |
| CRANE | Arc / orbit shot | Dolly on curved track (or stabilizer walk) circling the subject at constant radius | The world wheels around a fixed hero; background parallax streams past | Team-assembly beats, hero moments, physical product 360s *(Dark Knight standoffs)* | Full 180 orbit on curved track: she never moves; the war room revolves around her shoulders. |
| CRANE | Whip pan (swish pan) | Head whipped so fast the image smears; two whips cut together mid-blur hide the edit | Violent horizontal smear slingshots attention A→B; teleports across space with kinetic energy | Frenetic connective tissue, feature-to-feature tours *(La La Land)* | Whip pan off her grin — smear across the pit lane — land dead on the lights going green. |
| CRANE | Crash zoom (snap zoom) | Camera static; zoom barrel snapped in a fraction of a second — a lens event, not a camera move | Instant optical lunge; punchy, pulpy, an exclamation mark | Threat spotted, retro grindhouse flavor, sudden product detail *(Kill Bill)* | Double crash zoom: snap to the rearview eyes, snap again to the hand on the shifter. |
| CRANE | Slider micro-move | 2–4 ft rail on stands or tabletop; motorized drift of a few inches, subject often near-macro | Subtle floating parallax makes a static object feel sculptural, alive, expensive | Watches, bottles, tech hardware, food — luxury breathing room | Tabletop slider, three-inch drift: the watch crown crosses the light band, city bokeh sliding behind the crystal. |
| CRANE | Overhead god's-eye (true top-down) | Camera rigged at a true 90° straight down — ceiling grid, goalpost rig, or crane nose-down; safety-cabled over talent | Depth flattens; people become chess pieces on a plane — clinical, fate-like omniscience | Choreography and pattern (heists, kitchens, formations), transcendence, flat-lays *(BR2049)* | God's-eye from the grid: bodies and blueprints arranged like a diagram, her path through them a drawn line. |
| SPECIAL | Motion-control arm + high-speed (Bolt/cinebot class) | Programmed 6-axis robot arm on pedestal or rail, shutter-synced to a 1000-fps camera and to triggers — liquid dumps, lights, turntables; pixel-identical repeats | Standstill-to-violent whip and back in fractions of a second while the moment stretches razor-sharp; splash crowns orbited in perfect focus | Luxury macro (pours, shatters, cosmetics), frozen-athlete spots, multi-pass composites | Moco arm + 1000fps: whip 180° around the bottle at the exact frame the pour hits the glass — crown of droplets held mid-air, dead sharp. |
| SPECIAL | Bullet time (time-slice array) | An arc of dozens-to-hundreds of still cameras encircling the subject, fired in rapid sequence; frames interpolated into one traveling frozen moment | Time freezes while the viewpoint sweeps the frozen subject — physically impossible spectatorship | Signature action punctuation, examine-the-impact product beats *(The Matrix)* | 120-camera time-slice: freeze her mid-vault, orbit ninety degrees through the hanging dust, release time on landing. |
| SPECIAL | Rotating room / gimbal set | A practical set built inside a motorized rotating drum; camera bolted TO the set so the room looks still while gravity secretly rotates; actors time choreography to the roll | Gravity breaks on-screen — people walk walls, fights tumble along ceilings, no visible trick | Dream logic, zero-G space beats, world-turned-upside-down concepts *(Inception hallway)* | Rotating-set fight: camera locked to the corridor as it rolls — he brawls up the wall and across the ceiling in one take. |
| SPECIAL | Probe lens flythrough (snorkel) | Long thin tube lens with a tiny front element; the lens tip travels inside/through the subject while the camera body stays clear; wide-macro perspective, heavy light | Impossible interior journeys — through the mechanism, between chess pieces; bug-eye scale with wide-angle depth | Inside-the-product macro, food/drink internals, mechanism worship | Probe-lens pass: enter between the earcup grille slats, travel the driver coil, exit at the hinge as it folds. |
| SPECIAL | Split diopter | Half-moon close-focus glass over half the lens; near face and far figure sharp simultaneously, telltale soft seam between | In-camera split screen — threat and victim both tack-sharp; charged co-presence | Foreground product razor-sharp with story behind; paranoia, dramatic irony *(De Palma)* | Split diopter: the vial inches from lens, pin-sharp left; across the lab, the guard turning — equally sharp, right. |
| SPECIAL | Splash box / water-level housing (over-under) | Camera sealed behind a large dome port, floated at the surface so the waterline bisects the frame | Sunlit normality above the line, murk and threat below — swimmer's-eye vulnerability or predator POV | Dread-from-below, watersport ads, hidden-depths metaphors *(Jaws)* | Splash-box at the waterline: her stroke above the line, the reef falling to black below it, the lens half-drowned. |

## Corrections & provenance worth keeping

- *Children of Men*'s car oner is routinely miscited as Steadicam — it used a custom roof-mounted body rig (Doggicam class); the Bexhill battle was handheld. Canonical Steadicam oners: *Goodfellas*, *The Shining*, *Boogie Nights*.
- Dolly zoom invented by Paramount second-unit cameraman Irmin Roberts for *Vertigo*; "Bayhem" and "the Spielberg oner" coined/popularized by Every Frame a Painting; "Spielberg face" by Kevin B. Lee (2011); "planimetric" by David Bordwell; "double dolly" is Spike Lee's own production term; bullet time descends from Muybridge (1878) via Tim Macmillan's time-slice arrays.
- Useful opposed pairs when picking: Fincher (moves only with subject) vs Bay/Scott (never still); Kubrick/Anderson (centered grid) vs Kurosawa/Scott (layered telephoto chaos); Deakins/Villeneuve (subtractive light, negative space) vs Bayhem (additive flare, maximal frame).
- Lens shorthand: telephoto = compression, layering, surveillance (Kurosawa, Scott, Leone ECUs, Villeneuve distant wides); wide = immersion, entering the space (Kubrick pushes, Malick drifts, Spielberg oners).
